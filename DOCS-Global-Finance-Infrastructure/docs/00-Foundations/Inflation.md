# 07 - Inflation

## Documentation Overview

Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services in an economy over a period of time. It reduces the purchasing power of money, meaning each unit of currency buys fewer goods and services. This document covers the causes, measurement, types, effects, and policy responses to inflation, as well as the relationship between inflation and other economic variables.

## Definition and Measurement

### What is Inflation

Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services rises over time. It is measured as an annual percentage increase. When inflation occurs, each unit of currency buys fewer goods and services than it did previously.

How it works: If the inflation rate is 2% per year, an item that costs $100 today will cost approximately $102 next year. The purchasing power of $100 declines by 2% each year. Over a decade, the same $100 would buy only what $82 could buy at the start (with 2% annual inflation).

## Causes of Inflation

### Demand-Pull Inflation

Demand-pull inflation occurs when aggregate demand grows faster than aggregate supply, pulling prices upward. This is often described as "too much money chasing too few goods."

How it works: When consumers, businesses, and government collectively try to buy more than the economy can produce, prices rise. The gap between demand and supply creates upward pressure on prices. This typically occurs during economic booms, with low unemployment, high consumer confidence, and easy credit.

### Cost-Push Inflation

Cost-push inflation occurs when the costs of production increase, forcing producers to raise prices to maintain profit margins. Supply shocks are common triggers.

How it works: When input prices (wages, raw materials, energy) rise, producers face higher costs. They pass these costs to consumers through higher prices. Unlike demand-pull inflation, cost-push inflation occurs alongside falling output and rising unemployment (stagflation).

### Monetary Inflation

Monetary inflation occurs when the money supply grows faster than real economic output. According to the quantity theory of money, this is the primary cause of sustained inflation.

How it works: When central banks create money faster than the economy produces goods and services, the increased money supply chases the same amount of goods, driving up prices. Milton Friedman famously stated: "Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon."

```
QUANTITY THEORY OF MONEY

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                                                             │
    │   EQUATION: MV = PQ                                                        │
    │                                                                             │
    │   Where:                                                                   │
    │   M = Money Supply                                                         │
    │   V = Velocity of Money (average number of times each unit is spent)      │
    │   P = Price Level                                                          │
    │   Q = Real Output (quantity of goods and services)                        │
    │                                                                             │
    │                                                                             │
    │   ASSUMING V AND Q ARE CONSTANT IN THE SHORT RUN:                          │
    │                                                                             │
    │   %ΔM ≈ %ΔP (Inflation Rate)                                              │
    │                                                                             │
    │                                                                             │
    │   EXAMPLE:                                                                 │
    │                                                                             │
    │   Money supply increases by 10%                                            │
    │   Velocity constant                                                        │
    │   Real output constant (economy at full capacity)                          │
    │                                                                             │
    │   Result: Prices must rise by approximately 10%                           │
    │                                                                             │
    │                                                                             │
    │   MONETARY INFLATION PROCESS:                                              │
    │                                                                             │
    │   Central bank creates money                                              │
    │        │                                                                   │
    │        ▼                                                                   │
    │   Banks have more reserves                                                │

    │        ▼                                                                   │

    │        │                                                                   │
    │        ▼                                                                   │
    │   Borrowers spend newly created money                                     │
    │        │                                                                   │
    │        ▼                                                                   │
    │   Spending increases faster than production                               │
    │        │                                                                   │
    │        ▼                                                                   │
    │   Prices rise (inflation)                                                 │
    │                                                                             │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Inflation Expectations

Inflation expectations play a crucial role in determining actual inflation. If people expect higher inflation, they act in ways that make higher inflation happen.

How it works: Workers demand higher wages expecting higher prices. Businesses raise prices expecting higher costs. The central bank may accommodate these expectations by increasing money supply. Self-fulfilling prophecies can keep inflation high even after initial causes subside.

## Types of Inflation by Severity

### Creeping Inflation (1-3% per year)

Creeping inflation is low, predictable, and generally considered beneficial for a modern economy. Most central banks target this range.

How it works: Prices rise slowly and predictably. Consumers and businesses can plan for the future. Wages generally keep pace with inflation. Debtors benefit slightly as real debt declines slowly. Most economists consider 2% optimal.

### Walking Inflation (3-10% per year)

Walking inflation is moderate to high and begins to cause economic distortions. It is problematic but not yet destructive.

How it works: People begin to notice rising prices and may change behavior. They may buy goods sooner to avoid higher prices later (hoarding). Lenders demand higher interest rates. Cash holdings lose value noticeably. Wage negotiations become more difficult.

### Galloping Inflation (10-100% per year)

Galloping inflation is severe and destabilizing. Money rapidly loses value, and economic behavior changes dramatically.

How it works: People spend money immediately upon receiving it. Multiple price increases occur within months or weeks. Businesses may stop accepting local currency. Foreign currencies (US dollar, euro) become preferred for savings. The economy may contract as normal commerce breaks down.

### Hyperinflation (Over 50% per month)

Hyperinflation is an extreme, accelerating inflation that destroys the currency's value. Prices may double in days or hours.

How it works: Confidence in the currency collapses completely. People refuse to hold local money. Transactions shift to barter, foreign currency, or commodity money (gold, cigarettes). The velocity of money skyrockets as people spend instantly. The currency may become worthless, requiring replacement.

### On Debtors and Creditors

Inflation transfers wealth from creditors (lenders) to debtors (borrowers). Debt is repaid with money that is worth less than when it was borrowed.

How it works: If you borrow $100,000 at 5% interest and inflation runs at 5%, the real interest rate is 0%. You repay the loan with money that has the same purchasing power as when you borrowed. If inflation rises to 10% while your rate stays at 5%, your real interest rate becomes -5% (you repay with cheaper money).

Creditors lose purchasing power when inflation exceeds the interest rate they receive. Savers in bank accounts or bonds see real returns reduced or negative.


### On Fixed Income Groups

People on fixed incomes are harmed by inflation because their income does not increase when prices rise.

How it works: Retirees with fixed pensions see their purchasing power decline each year. Bondholders receiving fixed coupon payments experience lower real returns. Landlords with fixed-rate leases lose real income. Government benefit recipients may see delayed inflation adjustments (COLA).

### On Savers and Investors

Inflation erodes the real value of savings held in cash or low-interest accounts. Investors may seek inflation hedges.

How it works: Cash under the mattress loses value at the inflation rate. Savings accounts with interest below inflation lose purchasing power. Stocks may provide inflation protection if companies can raise prices. Real assets (real estate, commodities, inflation-linked bonds) preserve value during inflation.

### On Economic Efficiency

Inflation creates inefficiencies by distorting economic decisions.

Menu costs: The cost of changing prices (printing new menus, catalogs, price tags). During high inflation, businesses change prices frequently.

Shoe leather costs: Time and effort spent minimizing cash holdings (frequent trips to bank) to avoid inflation erosion.

Tax distortions: Inflation can push people into higher tax brackets even though real income hasn't increased (bracket creep).

Price signal distortion: Businesses may mistake price changes for changes in relative demand, leading to misallocation of resources.

## The Phillips Curve

### The Original Relationship

The Phillips Curve describes an inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment. Lower unemployment is associated with higher inflation, and vice versa.

How it works: When unemployment is low, workers have bargaining power and demand higher wages. Businesses raise prices to cover higher labor costs. Inflation rises. When unemployment is high, workers have less bargaining power, wage growth slows, and inflation falls.

```

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
```

### The Expectations-Augmented Phillips Curve

In the 1970s, the original Phillips Curve broke down as both inflation and unemployment rose together (stagflation). Economists added inflation expectations to explain this.

How it works: In the short run, the Phillips Curve exists because expectations are slow to adjust. In the long run, expectations adjust, and there is no trade-off. The natural rate of unemployment (NAIRU) is the rate at which inflation is stable.

```
EXPECTATIONS-AUGMENTED PHILLIPS CURVE

    │                                                                             │

    │   π = actual inflation                                                     │
    │   π^e = expected inflation                                                 │
    │   u = actual unemployment                                                  │

    │   β = sensitivity coefficient                                              │
```

### Inflation Targeting

Most major central banks use inflation targeting: announcing a specific inflation target (usually 2%) and adjusting policy to achieve it.

How it works: Central bank announces target (e.g., 2% CPI inflation). If inflation rises above target, central bank raises rates to cool economy. If inflation falls below target, central bank lowers rates to stimulate. Transparency and credibility are essential for effectiveness.

### Fiscal Policy Responses

Governments can use fiscal policy to fight inflation by reducing aggregate demand.

How it works: Reduce government spending (direct demand reduction). Increase taxes (reduces disposable income and private spending). Reduce budget deficit (less stimulus). In severe cases, impose wage and price controls (temporary, often ineffective long-term).

## Historical Inflation Episodes

### Zimbabwe Hyperinflation (2007-2009)

Zimbabwe experienced the second-worst hyperinflation in recorded history.

Causes: Land reforms disrupted agriculture (primary export), collapse in production, government printed money to pay expenses, political instability.

Consequences: 79.6 billion percent monthly inflation at peak (2008). Currency abandoned in 2009, replaced by US dollar and South African rand. 100 trillion Zimbabwe dollar notes became collector items worth less than $1 US.

### Recent Global Inflation (2021-2023)

Following the COVID-19 pandemic, global inflation surged to levels not seen since the 1980s.

Causes: Supply chain disruptions, pandemic stimulus spending, shift from services to goods, energy price spike after Russian invasion of Ukraine, tight labor markets.

Central bank responses: Rapid interest rate increases worldwide (Fed from 0% to 5.5%, ECB from -0.5% to 4.5%). Inflation began declining in 2023-2024.

## Formulas Quick Reference

```
INFLATION FORMULAS

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                                                             │
    │   Inflation Rate = (CPI_current - CPI_previous) / CPI_previous × 100       │
    │                                                                             │
    │   CPI = (Cost of basket current year / Cost of basket base year) × 100     │
    │                                                                             │
    │   GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100                            │
    │                                                                             │
    │   Real Interest Rate = Nominal Interest Rate - Inflation Rate              │
    │                                                                             │
    │   Real Value = Nominal Value / (1 + Inflation Rate)^Years                  │
    │                                                                             │
    │   Quantity Theory: MV = PQ                                                 │
    │                                                                             │
    │   %ΔM + %ΔV = %ΔP + %ΔQ (growth rates)                                     │
    │                                                                             │
    │   Phillips Curve: π = π^e - β(u - u_n) + ε                                 │
    │                                                                             │
    │   Fisher Equation: i = r + π^e                                             │
    │   (i = nominal rate, r = real rate, π^e = expected inflation)              │
    │                                                                             │
    │   Rule of 72 (price doubling time): Years to double = 72 / Inflation Rate  │
    │   Example: 8% inflation → 72/8 = 9 years to double prices                  │
    │                                                                             │
    │   Hyperinflation doubling time: Days to double = 72 / Monthly rate (%)     │
    │   Example: 50% monthly → 72/50 = 1.44 days (about 34 hours)                │
    │                                                                             │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Summary

1. Inflation is sustained increase in general price level. Reduces purchasing power of money.

2. Disinflation is slowing inflation rate. Deflation is falling prices (negative inflation).

3. CPI measures consumer prices. PPI measures producer prices. GDP deflator is broadest measure.

4. Demand-pull inflation: too much demand chasing too few goods.

5. Cost-push inflation: rising production costs force price increases (stagflation risk).

6. Monetary inflation: money supply grows faster than output (Quantity Theory: MV=PQ).

7. Inflation expectations can become self-fulfilling. Anchored expectations are crucial.

8. Creeping inflation (1-3%): Generally beneficial. Walking (3-10%): Problematic.

9. Galloping (10-100%): Destabilizing. Hyperinflation (>50% monthly): Currency collapse.

10. Effects: Creditors lose, debtors gain. Fixed income groups harmed. Savers lose purchasing power.

11. Real interest rate = Nominal rate - Inflation rate. Negative real rates transfer wealth to debtors.

12. Inflation hedges: TIPS, gold, real estate, commodities. Cash and fixed-rate bonds are poor hedges.

13. Costs: Menu costs, shoe leather costs, tax distortions, price signal distortion.

14. Phillips Curve: Short-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment. No long-run trade-off.

15. NAIRU: Natural rate of unemployment where inflation is stable. Below NAIRU, inflation rises.

16. Central banks control inflation through interest rates, OMO, reserve requirements, QT.

17. Inflation targeting (2%) adopted by most major central banks since 1990s.

18. Historical episodes: Germany 1923, US 1965-1982 (Volcker disinflation), Zimbabwe 2008, Global 2021-2023.
