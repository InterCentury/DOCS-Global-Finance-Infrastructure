# What Is Money

## Documentation Overview

Money is any item or verifiable record that is generally accepted as payment for goods and services and repayment of debts in a particular country or socio-economic context. While most people recognize money when they see it, a deeper understanding requires examining its functions, characteristics, forms, and evolution. This document covers the fundamental nature of money, its essential functions, the properties that make something suitable as money, and the different types of money that have existed throughout history.

## The Fundamental Nature of Money

### What Money Really Is

Money is a social and economic institution that facilitates exchange. It is not wealth itself but a tool for acquiring, measuring, and storing wealth. Money has value not because of its physical properties (in most cases) but because people believe it will be accepted by others in exchange for real goods and services.

How it works: Money solves the problem of barter by serving as an intermediary in exchange. Instead of finding someone who wants what you have and has what you want (double coincidence of wants), you sell what you have for money, then use that money to buy what you want. Money is accepted not for its own sake but for what it can buy.

## The Functions of Money

### Medium of Exchange

The most important function of money is serving as a medium of exchange. Money is anything that is widely accepted in exchange for goods and services.

How it works: Without money, trade requires barter: the direct exchange of one good for another. Barter requires a double coincidence of wants: each party must want what the other has at the same time. Money eliminates this requirement. You sell your labor or goods for money, then use that money to buy what you need. The seller accepts money not because they want money itself, but because they know they can use it to buy from others.

### Unit of Account

Money serves as a unit of account, providing a common measure of value for goods and services. It is the standard numerical unit for expressing prices and recording debts.

How it works: In a barter economy, each good has many prices: wheat priced in shoes, bread, meat, etc. This multiplicity makes comparison difficult. Money provides a single numeraire: all goods are priced in money units. You can immediately see that a $20 shirt is cheaper than a $50 shirt. You can calculate that a $30,000 car costs the same as 600 $50 shirts. This simplifies economic calculation.

### Store of Value

Money must serve as a store of value, meaning it can be saved and used for future purchases. Money should retain its purchasing power over time.

How it works: If you sell goods today, you may not want to spend the proceeds until next month or next year. Money must hold its value during that interval. If money rapidly loses value (inflation), people will spend it immediately rather than save. If money gains value (deflation), people will hoard it rather than spend. Moderate, stable inflation (2% target) balances storing value with encouraging productive investment.

### Standard of Deferred Payment

Money serves as a standard of deferred payment for future obligations. Debts and contracts are denominated in money units.

How it works: When you take out a mortgage, you promise to repay a certain number of dollars (or euros, yen, etc.) in the future. When a company issues a bond, it promises to pay interest and principal in money. When you sign a lease, you promise to pay rent in money. Money provides the unit for all credit transactions. This function requires that money be reasonably predictable in value over long periods.

## Types of Money

### Commodity Money

Commodity money is money that has intrinsic value or use value beyond its monetary function. The money itself is a useful good.

How it works: Gold can be used for jewelry or electronics. Salt can preserve food. Cattle provide meat, milk, and labor. The commodity has value even if never used as money. This intrinsic value provides a floor: the monetary value cannot fall below the commodity's use value.

Examples: Gold, silver, copper, salt, cattle, grain, cowrie shells, tea bricks, tobacco.

### Commodity-Backed Money (Representative Money)

Commodity-backed money is a claim on a commodity that can be redeemed for that commodity. The paper or token has no intrinsic value but represents a fixed quantity of a commodity stored elsewhere.

How it works: A bank issues paper notes that say "redeemable for 1 ounce of gold on demand." The note circulates as money because holders trust they can exchange it for gold. The gold sits in a vault, untouched. As long as not all note holders demand redemption at once, the system works.

Examples: Gold certificates, silver certificates, US dollars before 1971 (redeemable for gold to foreign central banks), banknotes under classical gold standard.

### Fiat Money

Fiat money has no intrinsic value and is not backed by any commodity. Its value derives from government decree (legal tender laws) and social trust.

How it works: The government declares that the currency must be accepted for payment of debts and taxes. Because taxes must be paid in the currency, people need to acquire it. Because everyone else accepts it, you accept it. The value is sustained by collective belief and legal enforcement.

Examples: All modern currencies: US dollar (since 1971), euro, Japanese yen, British pound, Chinese renminbi.

### Fiduciary Money

Fiduciary money is money that is accepted based on trust in the issuer, not by government decree. It circulates because people believe the issuer will honor its obligations.

How it works: A private bank issues notes that are not legal tender. People accept them because the bank has a reputation for redeeming them on demand. If trust fails, the money becomes worthless.

Examples: Banknotes before central bank monopolies, some local currencies, private cryptocurrencies, checks.

### Commercial Bank Money

Commercial bank money is the digital deposits held in bank accounts. Over 90% of money in modern economies exists in this form.

How it works: When you deposit physical cash, your bank credits your account with digital money. When you receive a direct deposit from your employer, digital money is created. When you write a check or use a debit card, digital money transfers between accounts. This money is a liability of the commercial bank, not the central bank.

Examples: Checking account balances, savings account balances, money market fund shares.

### Central Bank Money

Central bank money is the monetary base: physical currency (notes and coins) and commercial bank reserves held at the central bank.

How it works: Physical currency circulates among the public. Bank reserves are digital accounts that commercial banks hold at the central bank. They are used for interbank settlement and are the ultimate form of money in the economy (no credit risk).

Examples: Federal Reserve notes (dollar bills), bank reserves at the Fed, TARGET2 balances.

### M0 vs M1 vs M2 Relationships

How it works: M0 is created by the central bank (physical currency and reserves). M1 and M2 are created by commercial banks through fractional reserve lending. The relationship is:

M0 (Monetary Base) × Money Multiplier = M1 (Narrow Money)

The money multiplier is determined by the reserve requirement ratio and other factors.

## Liquidity and Moneyness

### The Concept of Liquidity

Liquidity is the ease with which an asset can be converted into a medium of exchange without significant loss of value. Money is the most liquid asset.

How it works: Cash is perfectly liquid: it is already money. A checking account is nearly perfectly liquid: you can spend it instantly. A savings account is slightly less liquid: you may need to transfer to checking. A stock is moderately liquid: you can sell it quickly but may lose value. Real estate is illiquid: selling takes months and may require a price discount.

### Near-Money

Near-money is an asset that is not money but can be quickly converted into money with low transaction costs and low risk of value loss.

How it works: Savings accounts, money market funds, and short-term government bonds are near-money. They are not directly spendable (you cannot buy groceries with a Treasury bill) but can be converted to cash in days with minimal loss.

## The Social and Legal Foundations

### Legal Tender

Legal tender laws specify which currency must be accepted for payment of debts.

How it works: The government declares that its currency is legal tender for all debts, public and private. A creditor cannot refuse legal tender and then sue for non-payment. This creates guaranteed demand for the currency and establishes its acceptability.

### Chartalism (State Theory of Money)

Chartalism is the theory that money derives its value from government acceptance of taxes in that money. Money is "a creature of the state."

How it works: The government requires taxes to be paid in its currency. This creates demand for the currency: people need the currency to pay taxes. The government then spends the currency into the economy. The cycle (taxes create demand, spending supplies currency) sustains the currency's value. This is also called the "tax-driven approach" to money.

### Metallism (Commodity Theory of Money)

Metallism is the theory that money derives its value from the commodity backing it, typically a precious metal.

How it works: A gold coin is valuable because it contains gold. A paper note is valuable because it is redeemable for gold. The value comes from the commodity, not the state. This theory dominated before 1971.

## Summary

1. Money is anything generally accepted as payment for goods, services, and debts.

2. Money is a social institution, not a physical thing. Its value depends on collective belief.

3. Money is not wealth. It is a claim on real goods and services.

4. Currency is the physical form of money (notes and coins). Wealth includes all assets.

5. Four functions: Medium of exchange, unit of account, store of value, standard of deferred payment.

6. Medium of exchange solves the double coincidence of wants problem in barter.

7. Unit of account provides a common measure of value, simplifying economic calculation.

8. Store of value allows saving and deferred consumption. Inflation erodes this function.

9. Standard of deferred payment enables credit, debt, and long-term contracts.

10. Sound money requires durability, portability, divisibility, uniformity, recognizability, scarcity, stability of value, and acceptability.

11. Commodity money has intrinsic value (gold, salt, cattle).

12. Commodity-backed money is a claim on a commodity (gold certificates).

13. Fiat money has no intrinsic value or backing. Value from legal tender and trust.

14. Fiduciary money depends on trust in private issuer (some cryptocurrencies).

15. Commercial bank money is digital deposits (90%+ of modern money supply).

16. Central bank money is physical currency and bank reserves (monetary base).

17. Hierarchy of money: Central bank money (highest) → commercial bank money → near-money → non-money assets.

18. Monetary aggregates: M0 (monetary base), M1 (narrow money), M2 (broad money).

19. Money multiplier: M1 = M0 × (1 + c) / (rr + c + e)

20. Liquidity: ease of converting an asset into money without value loss. Money is perfectly liquid.

21. Near-money: assets convertible to money quickly with low risk (savings deposits, money market funds).

22. Legal tender laws require acceptance of government currency for debts.

23. Chartalism: Money derives value from tax acceptance (state theory).

24. Metallism: Money derives value from commodity backing (gold standard theory).
