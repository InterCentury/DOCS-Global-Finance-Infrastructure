# Islamic Banking

## Documentation Overview

Islamic banking is a banking system that operates in accordance with the principles of Islamic law (Shariah). The core principle is the prohibition of interest (riba) and the requirement that all financial transactions involve real economic activity and risk-sharing. This document covers the fundamental principles, contract types, operational mechanisms, and key differences from conventional banking.

## Foundational Principles

### What is Islamic Banking

Islamic banking is a financial system that operates according to Islamic law (Shariah). The fundamental distinction from conventional banking is the prohibition of interest (riba). Instead of earning interest, Islamic banks earn profits through legitimate trade, investment, and risk-sharing activities.

How it works: An Islamic bank does not lend money for interest. Instead, it enters into partnership arrangements with customers, purchases assets and sells them at a markup, or leases assets for a rental fee. All transactions must be backed by real assets or services. Money itself is not a commodity to be bought and sold—it is only a medium of exchange.

## Core Contracts and Instruments

### Mudarabah (Profit-Sharing Partnership)

Mudarabah is a partnership where one party provides capital (rabb-ul-mal) and the other provides expertise and management (mudarib). Profits are shared according to a pre-agreed ratio, while losses are borne solely by the capital provider (unless due to negligence).

How it works: The bank provides 100% of the capital for a business venture. The customer (entrepreneur) manages the business. Profits are split according to a pre-agreed ratio (e.g., 60% bank, 40% entrepreneur). If the business loses money, the bank bears the loss. The entrepreneur loses only time and effort (unless negligent).

### Musharakah (Joint Venture Partnership)

Musharakah is a partnership where all partners contribute capital and share profits and losses according to their respective capital contributions or agreed ratios.

How it works: Bank and customer both contribute capital to a business venture. Both participate in management (or one may manage with consent). Profits are distributed according to the pre-agreed ratio (which may differ from capital contribution). Losses are shared strictly in proportion to capital contribution.

### Murabaha (Cost-Plus Sale)

Murabaha is a contract where the bank purchases an asset and sells it to the customer at a marked-up price, with payment deferred. The bank discloses the cost and profit margin.

How it works: Customer wants to buy equipment costing $100,000. Bank purchases the equipment from the supplier for $100,000. Bank sells it to the customer for $120,000 (cost + $20,000 profit) on deferred payment terms (e.g., 2 years). The bank's profit is the markup on the asset. The customer knows the cost and markup.

### Ijarah (Leasing)

Ijarah is a leasing contract where the bank purchases an asset and leases it to the customer for a specified period in exchange for rental payments. Ownership remains with the bank.

How it works: Bank buys a property or equipment. Bank leases it to customer for a monthly rental fee. Bank retains ownership and maintenance responsibility (in most cases). At the end of the lease term, the customer may purchase the asset (Ijarah wa Iqtina) or return it.

### Salam (Forward Sale)

Salam is a contract where the buyer pays the full price in advance for goods to be delivered at a future date. This facilitates agricultural and commodity financing.

How it works: Bank pays $10,000 to a farmer today for 1,000 bushels of wheat to be delivered in 6 months. The farmer uses the money for planting. The bank owns the right to receive wheat. The bank can sell the right to a third party (parallel Salam) or take delivery and sell in the market.

Islamic banks generate profit through various legitimate means, all based on real economic activity.

## Deposit Products

### Current Accounts (Wadiah)

Wadiah is a contract of safe-keeping where the bank is trusted to safeguard deposits and may use them with the depositor's consent.

How it works: Depositor places money in the bank for safekeeping. Bank guarantees the full amount (in nominal terms). Bank may use the funds for permissible activities. Bank pays no interest but may give gifts (hibah) at its discretion.

```
WADIAH DEPOSIT STRUCTURE

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐

    │   │                                                                     │   │
    │   │  Places deposit ─────────────────► Receives deposit                │   │
    │   │  Can withdraw anytime             Guarantees full amount            │   │
    │   │  No interest expected             May use funds permissibly         │   │
    │   │  May receive gift (hibah)         May give gift at discretion       │   │
    │   │                                                                     │   │
    │   └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘   │
    │                                                                             │
    │                                                                             │

    │   ├── Bank is custodian, not borrower                                     │
    │   ├── Depositor has full access to funds                                │
    │   ├── Bank can charge fees for services                                  │

    │   └── Principal fully guaranteed by bank                                │

    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
    ```

### Savings Accounts (Mudarabah)

Mudarabah savings accounts are profit-sharing accounts where depositors' funds are invested by the bank and profits are shared.

How it works: Depositor provides funds. Bank manages and invests the funds in Shariah-compliant ventures. Profits are shared according to a pre-agreed ratio (e.g., 60% depositor, 40% bank). Losses are borne by depositor (capital provider) unless due to bank negligence.

## Summary

1. Islamic banking operates according to Shariah principles, prohibiting riba (interest), gharar (excessive uncertainty), and maysir (gambling).

2. Core philosophy: risk-sharing, asset-backed transactions, and real economic activity.

3. Riba prohibition: money cannot generate money without productive activity. All profit must come from trade, leasing, or partnership.

4. Gharar prohibition: contracts must be transparent, with clear subject matter, price, and delivery terms.

5. Mudarabah: profit-sharing partnership where bank provides capital, entrepreneur provides management. Losses borne by capital provider.

6. Musharakah: joint venture partnership where all partners contribute capital and share profits/losses proportionally.

7. Murabaha: cost-plus sale where bank purchases asset and sells to customer at markup with deferred payment.

8. Ijarah: leasing contract where bank owns asset and leases to customer for rental payments.

9. Salam: forward sale with full payment today for future delivery (used for agricultural financing).

10. Istisna: manufacturing contract with staged payments (used for project and construction financing).

11. Islamic banks generate profit through: Murabaha markup, Ijarah rentals, Mudarabah profit shares, Musharakah dividends.

12. Shariah governance: Shariah Supervisory Board (SSB) approves products, issues fatwas, reviews operations.

13. Purification: non-compliant income must be identified and donated to charity.

14. Deposits: Wadiah (safe-keeping, principal guaranteed) and Mudarabah (profit-sharing, principal risk).

15. Conventional banking: interest-based, risk-transfer, can lend without asset backing.

16. Islamic banking: profit-based, risk-sharing, all transactions asset-backed.

17. Late payment penalties: cannot charge interest; may charge charity donation to discourage delay.

18. Prohibited investments: alcohol, pork, gambling, pornography, conventional banks, weapons.

19. Global presence: over 70 countries, $4 trillion in assets, fastest-growing banking segment.

20. Islamic banking is open to all (Muslims and non-Muslims) and is increasingly offered by conventional banks as an alternative window.
